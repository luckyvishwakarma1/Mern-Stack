About A.P.J.Abdul Kalam

A.P.J. Abdul Kalam was a prominent Indian scientist and the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007,
best known as the "Missile Man of India" for his work on India's ballistic missile and nuclear weapons programs.
Born in Rameswaram, he was instrumental in the development of India's first indigenous satellite launch vehicle
(SLV-III) and the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP), which created missiles like Agni and
Prithvi. He was a key figure in the 1998 Pokhran-II nuclear tests and a strong advocate for using technology to
transform India into a developed nation through his Technology Vision 2020 plan. After his presidency,
he continued to inspire students as a teacher and mentor until his death in 2015. Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO)

Scientific and Military Contributions:

Kalam was a visionary leader in India's defense and space sectors. He played a pivotal role in developing
India's first satellite launch vehicle, the SLV-III, which made India a member of the exclusive "Space Club".
As the chief of the IGMDP, he oversaw the development of crucial indigenous missiles, earning him the title
"Missile Man of India". He was also central to India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, which helped establish
India as a nuclear power.

Presidency and Vision:

As the 11th President of India, he was widely respected and nicknamed the "People's President"
for his ability connect with the public. During his term, he advanced his vision of a developed India with his "Technology Vision 2020"
plan, which focused on increasing agricultural productivity, technological growth, and better healthcare and education
access. Life after Presidency: Following his presidency, Kalam returned to his passion for teaching, serving as a visiting
professor at several prestigious institutions and continuing to mentor students across India. He was known for his simplicity,
integrity, and strong belief in the potential of the youth.
